Getting started

How the builder works

FilterBoxBuilder turns a few measurements into a complete, ready-to-make air-cleaner box built around standard rectangular furnace filters and off-the-shelf PC case fans.

Pick a fabrication method at the top — 3D print or Laser cut — then set your filter size, fan size and fan placement. The 3D preview updates live, the What you need panel lists parts, and the export button produces the files for your chosen method (a 3MF print kit, or an SVG cut sheet).

Everything is parametric: the housing is generated from your numbers, so any filter and fan combination produces a buildable box. The sections below explain each control. Most are in the main panel; a few live under Advanced.

Layout & design

Layout & design

Choose the overall shape of the box and where air moves through it.

Design presets

The Design dropdown loads a complete starting configuration (filter, fan and box settings) for a known build. Picking a preset fills in every field at once; editing any value afterwards switches the dropdown to Custom so you know you have diverged from the preset. Start from the preset closest to your parts, then fine-tune.

Filter layout

One side uses a single filter on one face of the box (a shallow panel or a deeper chamber). Both sides sandwiches the chamber between two filters for roughly double the filter area and clean-air delivery in the same footprint. 3D Print also offers Four sides — a tower with a filter on each wall and exhaust through the top.

More filter area lowers the air speed through the media, which raises efficiency and lowers noise (see MERV 13 & PC fans).

Fan placement & per-wall fan counts

The placement checkboxes choose which walls carry fans. Auto fills a wall with as many fans as physically fit; under Advanced you can instead set an exact count per wall (Left, Right, Top, Bottom, and Back). More fans raise airflow and clean-air delivery but draw more power and cost more; spreading them over several walls keeps each opening clear of the corners.

Fans are typically arranged to pull air in through the filter(s) and push it out the fan openings. Keeping intake (filter) area large relative to fan area keeps the system quiet.

Back fans (one-side box)

On a one-side layout, the wall opposite the filter is a solid plate that can carry its own grid of fans. Auto fills the plate with as many fans as fit; choose a fixed number if you prefer fewer. A fixed count is laid out as a centred, near-square block (for example 4 → 2×2) rather than bunched in a corner.

Turning Back on (with no wall fans) makes the box a shallow back-fan panel: the filter is on the front face and all the fans sit on the back plate. This is the classic single-filter wall-mount or window arrangement.

Box depth

For the one-side back-fan panel, Box depth sets the chamber depth — the gap between the filter and the back plate — in place of the fan-diameter chamber the deeper boxes use. A shallower box (the default is 70 mm) is more compact and wall-friendly; a deeper box gives the air a larger plenum, which can even out flow across the filter. It only applies when Back fans are on and no wall fans are selected.

Filter slot placement

Sets which wall the filter slides in through. The chosen wall gets the open loading slot so you can change the filter without taking the box apart; the other walls stay closed. Put the slot on whichever face is easiest to reach once the box is installed (often the back or a side).

Filter

Filter

The builder is sized around your actual filter — measure it, do not trust the nominal size.

Filter size (width, length, thickness)

Choose a stock size from the dropdown or enter your own. Filters are sold by a nominal size (e.g. 20"×20"×1") that is rounded up; the real part is a few millimetres smaller. Measure your filter and enter the true width, length and thickness in millimetres so the housing fits.

Thicker, deeper-pleated filters hold more media, so they capture more and clog more slowly — a 2" (≈50 mm) MERV 13 will out-perform and out-last a 1" filter at the same airflow. Use the swap button to exchange width and length if your filter is portrait vs landscape.

Filter fit clearance

Extra gap left around the filter footprint, per side, so it slides into the box without forcing. Increase it if your filter is a tight push or your material/print runs oversized; decrease it for a snugger, more airtight fit. A small clearance (≈1 mm) is usually enough; too much lets unfiltered air leak around the edges, so seal any gaps with foam tape if you open it up.

Filter rim

Width of the frame border that overlaps the edge of the filter and the air opening. The rim holds the filter in place and gives the frame rails their strength. A wider rim is sturdier but shrinks the open area the air passes through; keep it just wide enough to capture the filter gasket and carry the joints.

Fans

Fans

Standard PC case fans — quiet, cheap, PWM-controllable and easy to replace.

Fan size

The nominal diameter of the PC fans you will mount (commonly 120 mm or 140 mm). This sets the size of every fan opening and screw pattern and sizes the housing around them. 140 mm fans move the same air as 120 mm fans at a lower RPM, so they are usually quieter for the same airflow; 120 mm fans are easier to source and pack more densely. Match the value to fans you can actually buy.

Fan screw size

Diameter of each fan mounting-screw hole. PC fans use a roughly 4.3 mm hole for standard self-tapping fan screws; set this to match your screws (a touch smaller for self-tappers that bite into plywood or plastic, exact for machine screws and nuts). Four holes are placed at each fan on the standard fan bolt pattern.

Honeycomb grill (cell size & rib spacing)

An optional honeycomb pattern over a fan opening that protects fingers and the fan blades while passing air. Grill hex size is the cell width, flat-to-flat; Grill hex spacing is the rib (wall) thickness between cells. Bigger cells and thinner ribs let more air through with less noise; smaller cells are safer and stiffer. Ribs that are too thin may be fragile when printed or cut.

Cord pass-through

Cord pass-through

An optional bore for the fan power cable so the box can close up neatly.

Cord hole diameter

Diameter of the power-cord pass-through. Set it to fit your cable or barrel-jack grommet; set it to 0 for no cord hole at all. The builder keeps the bore clear of fan openings — if it would land under a fan, the fan bank re-packs to make room rather than moving the cord.

Power cord wall

Which wall the cord exits through. Choose the side that faces your outlet or hub once the box is placed.

Cord position

Slides the hole along the chosen wall — centre, or toward one end — so it lines up with your wiring.

Cord corner offset

Distance from the corner to the cord hole, used when the cord is positioned toward an end of the wall.

Material & joints

Material & joints (laser cut)

These tune the flat-pack joinery for laser-cut sheet stock. The defaults match the boxes.py generator the cut geometry is ported from.

Material thickness

Thickness of your sheet stock. This is the most important laser setting: it drives finger length, every slot width and the overall joinery. Measure your plywood or acrylic with calipers (nominal "3 mm" ply is often 2.7–3.2 mm) and enter the real value, or the joints will be loose or won't close.

Kerf / fit allowance

Compensation for the width the laser beam burns away (the kerf). The exported tool-path is offset by this amount so the finished part keeps its nominal size and the joints fit. Increase it if joints come out loose, decrease it if they are too tight to assemble. Typical values are around 0.05–0.15 mm depending on your laser and material; cut a test joint to dial it in.

Finger joints

The interlocking "comb" joints that hold the walls together. All five tuning values are multiples of the material thickness, so they scale with your stock. Defaults are sensible — only change them to suit a particular fit or look.

Finger width

Width of each finger, as a multiple of material thickness. Larger gives fewer, chunkier fingers; smaller gives more, finer ones.

Finger spacing

Gap between fingers, as a multiple of material thickness. Usually kept equal to the finger width for an even comb.

Finger hole width

Width of the slots that the mating fingers pass through, as a multiple of thickness. Affects how tightly fingers seat in their holes.

Finger hole offset

How far the finger-hole rows sit in from the panel edge, as a multiple of thickness. Controls how deep a lapped panel seats.

Finger play

Extra slack added to the finger slots for an easier fit, as a multiple of thickness. 0 is a tight press fit; add a little for hand assembly or glue gap.

Dovetails

The angled, self-locking joints used on the filter-frame rails. Again, sizes are multiples of material thickness.

Dovetail size

Tooth size, as a multiple of material thickness. Larger teeth are stronger and easier to align; smaller teeth give more of them.

Dovetail depth

How far the dovetails interlock, as a multiple of thickness.

Dovetail taper

Flare angle of the dovetail, in degrees. Steeper locks harder and resists pull-apart, but is tighter to assemble and less forgiving of kerf error.

Drawing & output

Drawing & output

Engrave part labels

Etches each part's name lightly onto the cut sheet so you can tell the pieces apart while assembling. Turn it off for a clean, unmarked cut. Engraving adds laser time; place labels on what will be the inside face if you want them hidden.

Reference scale

Draws a ruler of a known length on the sheet so you can confirm your cut or export came out at 1:1 before committing material. After importing the SVG, measure the reference line; if it is off, fix your document units or printer scaling.

Chunk labels (3D print)

When a part is too big for the print bed it is split into chunks. Chunk labels deboss a short matching code on each piece so you can line up the right chunks when gluing. Only relevant to prints split into multiple pieces.

Build it

Fabrication, gluing & assembly

The same box can be made two ways. Pick the workflow that matches your tools, then follow the tips for that method.

A. Laser-cut plywood (or acrylic)

Cutting

  • Use flat, void-free plywood — Baltic birch 3 mm or 6 mm is ideal. Warped stock fights the joints.
  • Measure thickness with calipers and set Material thickness to the real value.
  • Cut one test finger joint first and tune Kerf / fit allowance until the joint taps together snugly without forcing.
  • Keep Engrave labels on for your first build; mask or sand off scorch with a light pass of fine sandpaper.

Dry-fit, then glue

  • Assemble the whole box without glue first to confirm every joint and the filter slot work.
  • Glue with wood PVA (e.g. Titebond) for plywood, or acrylic cement for acrylic. Apply a thin bead inside the finger slots, not on the faces.
  • Leave the filter loading slot wall un-glued (or make it a removable panel) so you can change filters.
  • Square the box against a flat surface and clamp or tape lightly; wipe squeeze-out before it cures.
  • Seal any small gaps around the filter with self-adhesive foam weatherstrip so all the air goes through the media, not around it.

B. 3D-printed with alignment pins

Printing

  • Print in PLA, PETG or ASA. PETG/ASA tolerate heat and sunlight better for a unit left running.
  • Walls of 2–3 mm (3–4 perimeters) are plenty; the box carries little load. 15–20% infill is fine.
  • Large parts are auto-split into bed-sized chunks. Keep chunk labels on so the matching codes print on each piece.
  • Orient for strength across the layer lines at the joints; the exporter auto-orients chunks to minimise supports.

Alignment pins & gluing

  • Split faces meet on printed alignment pins and matching holes — dry-fit them to confirm the chunks seat flush before glue.
  • Glue chunk seams with cyanoacrylate (CA/super glue) for PLA/PETG, or a plastic solvent/epoxy for ASA. The pins hold registration while the glue sets.
  • Match the chunk codes so adjacent pieces are the pair they were generated as.
  • Test-fit the filter and fans before final gluing; leave the filter-access face removable.

Mounting the fans (both methods)

  • Fit fans so they pull air through the filter and exhaust out the fan openings (check the arrow on the fan hub).
  • Use the four fan screw holes per fan; self-tapping fan screws bite directly into plywood or plastic.
  • Wire PWM fans to a 12 V supply or fan hub sized for the total current; route the cable through the cord pass-through.
  • Add the optional honeycomb grill or a snap-on finger guard on any exposed fan.

Why this works

Why MERV 13 and PC fans

A box of MERV 13 filter media driven by several quiet PC fans — sometimes called a "filter cube" or PC-fan filter box — is a cost-effective, high-throughput indoor air cleaner. Here is the reasoning, with sources.

What MERV 13 means

MERV (Minimum Efficiency Reporting Value) comes from the ANSI/ASHRAE Standard 52.2 test, which measures how well a filter removes particles across twelve size bins from 0.3 to 10 µm and reports a single 1–16 rating [1]. A MERV 13 filter captures a large share of the fine (PM2.5) particles that matter for smoke, allergens and respiratory aerosols — independent testing shows roughly 50% capture at the hardest ~0.35 µm size, rising to ~85–90% by 1 µm [2].

Throughput beats peak efficiency: why MERV 13 can out-clean HEPA

A room's cleanup speed is its Clean Air Delivery Rate (CADR) — the volume of fully filtered air delivered per minute — defined by the ANSI/AHAM AC-1 standard [3]. CADR is efficiency × airflow, so airflow matters as much as the percentage captured.

True HEPA media is ≥99.97% efficient at 0.3 µm but is dense, so it has a high pressure drop and chokes airflow [4]. A MERV 13 filter passes far more air for the same fan, and research shows that increasing filter efficiency past a point does not raise CADR because the pressure penalty cancels the gain [5]. By spreading MERV 13 media over a large area (multiple filters) and pushing modest airflow through all of it, a filter box delivers a very high CADR cheaply: a five-filter DIY box-fan unit was measured at roughly 1,000–1,440 m³/h (≈600–850 CFM) — several times a typical portable HEPA purifier [2]. The U.S. EPA studied these DIY cleaners and found a MERV 13 box fan filter is an effective, low-cost way to reduce indoor wildfire smoke [6, 7], and recommends MERV 13 as the minimum for smoke and airborne pathogens [6].

Rule of thumb: a lot of air through "good enough" media usually cleans a room faster than a little air through "perfect" media. Large filter area + several fans = high CADR at low noise.

Why PC fans: low noise, control and maintainability

Because the box uses a large filter area, each fan only has to overcome a low pressure drop — exactly the regime where PC case fans shine. Quality 120/140 mm PWM fans move 45–70 CFM at roughly 12–25 dBA [8], far quieter than a box fan at full tilt, and 140 mm fans hit a given airflow at lower RPM, so they are quieter still for the same output. Other advantages:

  • Speed control: 4-pin PWM lets you trade noise for airflow, or run quietly overnight.
  • Low, safe voltage: 12 V DC fans are simple to wire and have no mains heat risk in an always-on unit.
  • Maintainable & cheap: fans are standardised, inexpensive and individually replaceable; filters are commodity furnace filters you swap on a schedule.
  • Redundancy: several small fans keep working (at reduced output) if one fails.

Replace filters when airflow noticeably drops or per the filter maker's guidance — heavy smoke can load a MERV 13 in weeks. A clogged filter raises pressure drop and lowers CADR, so timely changes keep the box performing.
