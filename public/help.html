Why a MERV 13 PC-fan filter box

A box of MERV 13 filter media driven by a few quiet PC fans is often called a "filter cube" or PC-fan filter box. It is one of the cheapest, highest-airflow air cleaners you can build or buy. Here is what it is and why it works, with sources.

PHOTO PLACEHOLDER: a finished 3D-printed filter box running in a room.

The idea

Furnace filters are inexpensive, easy to find, and surprisingly effective. Mount several of them in a box. Pull air through all of them at once with a bank of computer fans. The result is a large, quiet, low-cost air cleaner. Because the filter area is large, the air passes through the media slowly. That keeps the capture rate high and the noise low, while the total airflow stays high.

What MERV 13 means

MERV stands for Minimum Efficiency Reporting Value. It comes from the ASHRAE 52.2 test [1]. The test measures how well a filter traps particles from 0.3 to 10 µm, across twelve size groups. It then reports a single score from 1 to 16. A MERV 13 filter captures most of the fine (PM2.5) particles that matter for smoke, pollen, and germs. Independent testing shows roughly half of the hardest 0.35 µm size, rising to about 85 to 90% by 1 µm [2].

Airflow beats peak rating: why MERV 13 can out-clean HEPA

How quickly a room is cleaned is its Clean Air Delivery Rate, or CADR. It is defined by the AHAM AC-1 standard [3]. CADR is the capture rate multiplied by the airflow. So airflow matters just as much as the percentage the filter traps. True HEPA media captures 99.97% at 0.3 µm. But it is dense, so it restricts airflow and the fan moves less air [4]. A MERV 13 filter lets far more air through for the same fan. In fact, a denser, higher-rated filter does not always raise CADR, because the lost airflow can cancel the gain [5].

The trick is to use a large area of MERV 13 media. Then push steady airflow through all of it. A five-filter DIY box-fan unit was measured at roughly 600 to 850 CFM (1,000 to 1,440 m³/h). That is several times the output of a typical portable HEPA unit [2]. The U.S. EPA tested these DIY cleaners. It found that a MERV 13 box-fan filter is a cheap, effective way to reduce indoor wildfire smoke [6, 7]. The EPA recommends at least MERV 13 for smoke and airborne germs [6].

Rule of thumb: a lot of air through "good enough" media usually cleans a room faster than a little air through "perfect" media. A large filter area plus several fans gives a high CADR at low noise.

Why PC fans: quiet, controllable, and easy to maintain

The box has a large filter area, so each fan only has to overcome a little resistance. That is exactly where PC case fans excel. Good 120 or 140 mm PWM fans move 45 to 70 CFM at roughly 12 to 25 dBA [8]. That is far quieter than a box fan at full speed. 140 mm fans reach a given airflow at a lower speed, so they are quieter still. Other advantages:

  • Speed control: a 4-pin PWM fan lets you trade noise for airflow, or run it quietly overnight.
  • Safe, low voltage: 12 V DC fans are simple to wire and stay cool.
  • Cheap and repairable: fans are standardised and easy to swap. The filters are common furnace filters you replace on a schedule.
  • Redundancy: if one fan fails, the rest keep running.

Replace the filters when the airflow noticeably drops, or whenever the maker recommends. Heavy smoke can clog a MERV 13 within weeks. A clogged filter restricts airflow and lowers CADR, so timely changes keep the box performing.

Using the builder

How the builder works

FilterBoxBuilder turns a few measurements into a complete air-cleaner box. It is built around standard furnace filters and off-the-shelf PC fans.

PHOTO PLACEHOLDER: screenshot of the builder: 3D view on the left, controls on the right.

Choose a method at the top: 3D print or Laser cut. Then set your filter size, fan size, and fan placement. The 3D view updates as you work. The What you need panel lists the parts. The export button produces the files for your method: a 3MF print kit, or an SVG cut sheet.

You set the numbers, and the box is generated to match. So any filter and fan combination produces a box you can build. The sections below explain each control. Most are in the main panel, and a few are under Advanced.

Layout & design

Layout & design

Choose the overall shape of the box and where the air moves through it.

PHOTO PLACEHOLDER: 3D view of the three layouts: one-side, both-sides (sandwich), and the four-side tower.

Design presets

The Design menu loads a complete setup for a known build: the filter, the fans, and the box. Picking one fills in every field at once. Changing any value switches the menu to Custom, so you know you have left the preset. Start with the preset closest to your parts, then fine-tune.

Filter layout

One side places a single filter on one face of the box. Both sides puts a filter on two faces, for roughly twice the filter area and airflow in the same footprint. 3D Print also offers Four sides: a tower with a filter on each wall and exhaust through the top.

More filter area slows the air through the media. That raises the capture rate and lowers noise (see Why a MERV 13 PC-fan filter box).

Fan placement & per-wall fan counts

The checkboxes choose which walls carry fans. Auto fills a wall with as many fans as physically fit. Under Advanced you can instead set an exact count per wall: Left, Right, Top, Bottom, and Back. More fans mean more airflow, but more cost and power. Spread them across several walls to keep each opening clear of the corners.

Fans are usually arranged to pull air in through the filters and push it out through the fan openings. Keep the filter area large relative to the fan area to stay quiet.

Back fans (one-side box)

On a one-side box, the wall opposite the filter is a solid plate. It can carry its own grid of fans. Auto fills the plate, or you can choose a fixed number for fewer. A fixed count is laid out as a centred, near-square block (for example, 4 becomes 2×2), not bunched into a corner.

Turn Back on with no wall fans, and the box becomes a shallow back-fan panel. The filter is on the front, and all the fans sit on the back plate. This is the classic single-filter wall-mount or window arrangement.

Box depth

On the one-side back-fan panel, Box depth sets how deep the box is. It is the gap between the filter and the back plate, used instead of the fan-diameter chamber. The default is 70 mm. A shallow box is more compact and easy to wall-mount. A deeper box gives the air a larger plenum, which can even out the flow. It only applies when Back fans are on and no wall fans are selected.

Filter slot placement

This sets which wall the filter slides in through. That wall gets the open loading slot, so you can change the filter without taking the box apart. The other walls stay closed. Put the slot on whichever face is easiest to reach once the box is installed, often the back or a side.

Filter

Filter

The box is sized to your actual filter, so measure it rather than trusting the nominal size.

Filter size (width, length, thickness)

Choose a stock size from the menu, or enter your own. Filters are sold by a rounded nominal size, such as 20"×20"×1". But the real part is a few millimetres smaller. So measure your filter, and enter the true width, length, and thickness in millimetres so the housing fits. Thicker filters with deeper pleats hold more media, so they capture more and clog more slowly. A 2" (about 50 mm) MERV 13 outperforms and outlasts a 1" filter at the same airflow. Use the swap button to exchange width and length.

Filter fit clearance

This is the extra gap left around the filter, per side, so it slides in without forcing. Increase it if the filter is a tight push, or if your parts come out slightly oversized. Decrease it for a snugger, more sealed fit. About 1 mm is usually enough. Too much clearance lets unfiltered air slip past the edges, so seal any gaps with foam tape.

Filter rim

This is the width of the frame border that overlaps the filter and the air opening. The rim holds the filter and gives the frame its strength. A wider rim is sturdier, but it covers more of the open area. Keep it just wide enough to capture the filter and carry the joints.

Fans

Fans

Standard PC case fans: quiet, inexpensive, easy to control, and easy to replace.

PHOTO PLACEHOLDER: a 140 mm PC fan next to a fan opening with the honeycomb grill.

Fan size

This is the diameter of the PC fans you will use, commonly 120 or 140 mm. It sets each fan opening and screw pattern, and sizes the housing around them. 140 mm fans move the same air at a lower speed, so they tend to be quieter. 120 mm fans are easier to source and pack more densely. Match this to fans you can actually buy.

Fan screw size

This is the diameter of each fan mounting-screw hole. PC fans use roughly a 4.3 mm hole for standard fan screws. Match it to your screws: a little smaller for self-tappers that bite into plywood or plastic, exact for machine screws and nuts. Four holes are placed at each fan in the standard pattern.

Honeycomb grill (cell size & rib spacing)

This is an optional honeycomb pattern over a fan opening. It guards fingers and the blades while letting air through. Grill hex size is the cell width, measured flat to flat. Grill hex spacing is the rib thickness between cells. Larger cells and thinner ribs let more air through with less noise. Smaller cells are safer and stiffer. Ribs that are too thin may be fragile.

Cord pass-through

Cord pass-through

An optional bore for the fan power cable, so the box can close up neatly.

Cord hole diameter

This is the diameter of the power-cord pass-through. Set it to fit your cable or barrel-jack grommet. Set it to 0 for no hole at all. The builder keeps the bore clear of the fan openings. If it would land under a fan, the fan bank re-packs to make room, rather than moving the cord.

Power cord wall

This is the wall the cord exits through. Choose the side that faces your outlet or hub once the box is placed.

Cord position

This slides the hole along the chosen wall (centre, or toward one end) so it lines up with your wiring.

Cord corner offset

This is the distance from the corner to the cord hole. It is used when the cord is positioned toward one end of the wall.

Material & joints

Material & joints (laser cut)

These settings tune the flat-pack joinery for laser-cut sheet stock. The defaults match the boxes.py generator the cut geometry is ported from.

Material thickness

This is the thickness of your sheet stock. It is the most important laser setting, because it drives the finger length and every slot width. Measure your plywood or acrylic with calipers. (Nominal "3 mm" ply is often 2.7 to 3.2 mm.) Enter the real value, or the joints will be loose or will not close.

Kerf / fit allowance

This compensates for the width the laser beam burns away, known as the kerf. The exported tool-path is offset by this amount, so the finished part keeps its size and the joints fit. Increase it if joints come out loose. Decrease it if they are too tight to assemble. Most lasers want roughly 0.05 to 0.15 mm, so cut a test joint to find yours.

Finger joints

These are the interlocking comb joints that hold the walls together. All five values are multiples of the material thickness, so they scale with your stock. The defaults work well. Change them only to suit a particular fit or appearance.

Finger width

The width of each finger, as a multiple of thickness. Larger gives fewer, wider fingers. Smaller gives more, finer ones.

Finger spacing

The gap between fingers, as a multiple of thickness. It is usually kept equal to the finger width, for an even comb.

Finger hole width

The width of the slots the mating fingers pass through, as a multiple of thickness. It determines how tightly the fingers seat.

Finger hole offset

How far the finger-hole rows sit in from the panel edge, as a multiple of thickness. It controls how deeply a lapped panel seats.

Finger play

Extra slack added to the slots for an easier fit, as a multiple of thickness. 0 is a tight press fit. Add a little for hand assembly or a glue gap.

Dovetails

These are the angled, self-locking joints on the filter-frame rails. They are again sized as multiples of the material thickness.

Dovetail size

The tooth size, as a multiple of thickness. Bigger teeth are stronger and easier to align. Smaller teeth give you more of them.

Dovetail depth

How far the dovetails interlock, as a multiple of thickness.

Dovetail taper

The flare angle, in degrees. A steeper angle locks harder. But it is tighter to assemble and less forgiving of kerf error.

Drawing & output

Drawing & output

PHOTO PLACEHOLDER: the Laser drawing (cut sheet) view, with engraved labels and the reference scale.

Engrave part labels

This etches each part's name lightly onto the cut sheet, so you can tell the pieces apart while assembling. Turn it off for a clean cut. Engraving adds laser time, so place labels on the inside face if you want them hidden.

Reference scale

This draws a ruler of a known length on the sheet. You can then confirm your cut came out at 1:1 before committing material. After importing the SVG, measure the line. If it is off, fix your document units or printer scaling.

Chunk labels (3D print)

When a part is too big for the print bed, it is split into chunks. Chunk labels deboss a short matching code on each piece, so you can line them up when gluing. This only matters for prints split into multiple pieces.

Build it

Fabrication, gluing & assembly

You can make the same box two ways. Pick the workflow that fits your tools, then follow its tips.

A. Laser-cut plywood (or acrylic)

Cutting

  • Use flat, void-free plywood. Baltic birch 3 mm or 6 mm is ideal, because warped stock fights the joints.
  • Measure the thickness with calipers, and set Material thickness to the real value.
  • Cut one test finger joint first. Tune Kerf / fit allowance until it taps together snugly without forcing.
  • Keep Engrave labels on for your first build. Sand off any scorch with a light pass of fine sandpaper.

Dry-fit, then glue

  • Assemble the whole box without glue first, to confirm every joint and the filter slot work.
  • Glue plywood with wood PVA (such as Titebond), or acrylic with acrylic cement. Apply a thin bead inside the slots, not on the faces.
  • Leave the filter-slot wall un-glued, or make it a removable panel, so you can change filters.
  • Square the box against a flat surface, and clamp or tape it lightly. Wipe off any squeeze-out before it cures.
  • Seal small gaps around the filter with self-adhesive foam weatherstrip. Then all the air passes through the media, not around it.

B. 3D-printed with alignment pins

Printing

  • Print in PLA, PETG, or ASA. PETG and ASA tolerate heat and sunlight better for a unit left running.
  • Walls of 2 to 3 mm (3 to 4 perimeters) are plenty, since the box carries little load. 15 to 20% infill is fine.
  • Large parts are split into bed-sized chunks. Keep chunk labels on, to print the matching codes on each piece.
  • Orient parts so the joints are strong across the layer lines. The exporter auto-orients chunks to minimise supports.

Alignment pins & gluing

  • Split faces meet on printed alignment pins and matching holes. Dry-fit them to confirm the chunks seat flush before gluing.
  • Glue chunk seams with cyanoacrylate (super glue) for PLA and PETG, or a plastic solvent or epoxy for ASA. The pins hold registration while the glue sets.
  • Match the chunk codes, so each pair goes together as it was generated.
  • Test-fit the filter and fans before final gluing. Leave the filter face removable.

Mounting the fans (both methods)

  • Position fans to pull air through the filter and exhaust out the fan openings. Check the arrow on the fan hub.
  • Use the four fan-screw holes per fan. Self-tapping fan screws bite directly into plywood or plastic.
  • Wire PWM fans to a 12 V supply or hub sized for the total current. Route the cable through the cord pass-through.
  • Add the optional honeycomb grill or a snap-on finger guard on any exposed fan.
